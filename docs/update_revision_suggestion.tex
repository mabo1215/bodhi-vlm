\section*{Overall Recommendation}

\textbf{Recommendation: Major Revision (not ready for acceptance in current form).}

The manuscript addresses a timely and potentially important problem: \emph{post hoc feature-level auditing of privacy-budget alignment} in vision backbones and VLM vision encoders. The paper is improved relative to a more over-claimed framing because it now explicitly states that Bodhi VLM is \emph{not} a formal DP certifier and that the results are restricted to \emph{feature-level auditing under controlled additive perturbation models}. This narrowing is appropriate.

However, at the standard of \emph{IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks and Learning Systems}, the paper still has several serious weaknesses that prevent acceptance in its current form. The most important issues are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item the paper still does not establish a sufficiently strong \textbf{task definition and evaluation target};
\item the \textbf{mathematical role of EMPA} remains only partially justified;
\item the \textbf{experimental evidence is not strong enough} to support the claimed usefulness of the method;
\item the \textbf{statistical protocol is inadequate} for a top-tier journal;
\item the \textbf{baselines are weak or not truly task-matched};
\item several \textbf{notation, reproducibility, and caption-level clarity} issues remain.
\end{enumerate}

In short, the paper has a publishable core idea, but it still reads more like an exploratory framework paper than a rigorously validated TNNLS article.

\section*{Summary of the Paper}

The paper proposes \emph{Bodhi VLM}, a feature-level privacy-budget auditing framework for vision backbones and VLM vision encoders. The framework consists of three modules:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{BUA} (Bottom-Up Analysis), which partitions layer-wise features into sensitive and non-sensitive groups from low to high layers;
\item \textbf{TDA} (Top-Down Analysis), which performs an analogous grouping from high to low layers;
\item \textbf{EMPA} (Expectation-Maximization Privacy Assessment), which fits a mixture model to sensitive-group perturbed features and compares the fitted distribution against a reference perturbation pattern under a declared privacy budget $\epsilon$.
\end{enumerate}

The experiments cover detector backbones (YOLOv4, PPDPTS, DETR, MDCRF+YOLO) and VLM vision encoders (CLIP, LLaVA, BLIP), mainly under synthetic Gaussian or Laplace perturbations with scale proportional to $1/\epsilon$.

\section*{Strengths}

\begin{enumerate}
\item The paper tackles an interesting and underexplined practical question: how to \emph{audit} whether observed perturbation in intermediate representations is aligned with a declared privacy budget.
\item The manuscript now makes an important distinction between \emph{empirical budget-alignment auditing} and \emph{formal differential privacy guarantees}, which is necessary and welcome.
\item The effort to unify detector backbones and VLM visual encoders under one layer-wise grouping pipeline is conceptually interesting.
\item The BUA/TDA decomposition is intuitive and potentially useful as a general feature attribution/grouping scaffold.
\item The paper includes algorithms, explicit equations, and a reproducibility-oriented table, which is the correct direction.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Major Concerns}

\subsection*{1. The paper still lacks a sharp and fully defensible target of estimation}

\textbf{Problem.}  
The manuscript is now more careful than before, but the exact object being estimated is still not sufficiently precise. In Section III-A and Section III-G, the paper alternates between terms such as \emph{budget-alignment score}, \emph{privacy discrepancy signal}, \emph{bias}, \emph{rMSE to a reference}, and \emph{assessment under declared budget $\epsilon$}. These are not interchangeable quantities.

At present, the paper defines
\[
A(\tilde f, f, S_\epsilon, \epsilon)
\]
as a scalar assessment signal, and later defines
\[
\mathrm{BAS}_\epsilon := d\!\left(\Theta_\epsilon, \Theta_\epsilon^{\mathrm{ref}}\right),
\qquad
\mathrm{Bias}_\epsilon := \left\|\lambda_\epsilon - \lambda_\epsilon^{\mathrm{ref}}\right\|_2.
\]
But the paper does not fully specify which of these is the \emph{primary target} of the method, how these quantities relate to each other across all tables/figures, and which quantity should be interpreted as the actual output of Bodhi VLM.

This matters because the empirical story changes depending on the chosen target. In particular, Section IV-B.4 explicitly shows that the EMPA bias is largely insensitive to $\epsilon$ in the synthetic setting, which weakens its use as a budget-sensitive auditing quantity.

\textbf{Location.}  
Section III-A, Section III-G, Table III, Table IV, Table V, Table VII, Section IV-B.4, Section IV-E(5)--(7).

\textbf{Why this matters technically.}  
A TNNLS paper must define a primary learning/inference objective with clear semantics. Here, the method appears to output at least three different quantities:

\begin{align}
A(\tilde f,f,S_\epsilon,\epsilon) &\in \mathbb{R}, \\
\mathrm{BAS}_\epsilon &= d\!\left(\Theta_\epsilon,\Theta_\epsilon^{\mathrm{ref}}\right), \\
\mathrm{Bias}_\epsilon &= \left\|\lambda_\epsilon-\lambda_\epsilon^{\mathrm{ref}}\right\|_2.
\end{align}

The paper must decide whether:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\mathrm{BAS}_\epsilon$ is the core method output and $\mathrm{Bias}_\epsilon$ is a restricted diagnostic;
\item $\mathrm{Bias}_\epsilon$ is the actual reported estimator;
\item or both are secondary diagnostics around a broader auditing objective.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Required fix.}  
Add a short subsection that defines the \emph{primary} output, the \emph{secondary} diagnostics, and the exact use of each in experiments. For example:

\begin{verbatim}
\subsubsection*{Primary and secondary outputs of Bodhi VLM}
The primary output of Bodhi VLM is the budget-alignment score
\[
\mathrm{BAS}_\epsilon = d(\Theta_\epsilon,\Theta_\epsilon^{\mathrm{ref}}),
\]
which compares the fitted sensitive-feature distribution to a
reference sensitive-feature distribution under the same declared
budget and perturbation family.
The quantity
\[
\mathrm{Bias}_\epsilon = \|\lambda_\epsilon-\lambda_\epsilon^{\mathrm{ref}}\|_2
\]
is used only as a restricted weight-only diagnostic and should not
be interpreted as the main output when means and variances are
included in $\Theta_\epsilon$.
\end{verbatim}

\subsection*{2. EMPA is operationally clearer than before, but still not mathematically strong enough for a top-tier method paper}

\textbf{Problem.}  
Section III-G is improved because it now provides an explicit diagonal-Gaussian mixture model:
\[
p(v \mid \Theta_\epsilon)
=
\sum_{k=1}^{K}
\lambda_k
\mathcal{N}\!\left(v;\mu_k,\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_k^2)\right).
\]
This is a positive step. However, the paper still does not adequately justify \emph{why} this model is the correct or even a plausible representation for the sensitive-feature distribution under perturbation. The choice of $K=4$, diagonal covariance, and the reference comparison on the same feature subset appears ad hoc. The method therefore reads more like a heuristic scoring procedure than a principled estimator.

Moreover, the paper does not analyze the sensitivity of the discrepancy score to:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the number of mixture components $K$;
\item diagonal versus full covariance;
\item feature normalization;
\item pooling into a ``common feature space'';
\item layer selection and aggregation.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Location.}  
Section III-G, Table II, Section IV-A.4, Table IV.

\textbf{Why this matters technically.}  
If the proposed assessment score is heavily dependent on arbitrary mixture design choices, then the empirical conclusions may not reflect the privacy-budget alignment problem itself, but instead the inductive bias of the chosen density model.

\textbf{Required fix.}  
Add a robustness subsection that studies:
\[
K \in \{2,4,8\},
\]
and compares at least:
\[
\Sigma_k = \operatorname{diag}(\sigma_k^2)
\quad \text{vs.} \quad
\Sigma_k \text{ full}.
\]
Also show whether the score changes materially under:
\[
d(\Theta_\epsilon,\Theta_\epsilon^{\mathrm{ref}})
=
\left\|
\phi(\Theta_\epsilon)-\phi(\Theta_\epsilon^{\mathrm{ref}})
\right\|_2
\]
with
\[
\phi(\Theta)
=
[\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_K,\mu_1,\dots,\mu_K,\sigma_1^2,\dots,\sigma_K^2]
\]
versus a weight-only version.

A concrete patch would be:

\begin{verbatim}
\paragraph*{EMPA robustness to mixture design.}
We evaluate the stability of EMPA under different mixture
specifications by varying the number of components
$K\in\{2,4,8\}$ and the covariance model
(diagonal vs.\ full covariance). We additionally compare
three discrepancy variants:
\[
d_\lambda,\quad d_{\lambda,\mu},\quad d_{\lambda,\mu,\sigma^2},
\]
where each variant uses only the corresponding subset of
mixture parameters. This analysis determines whether the
reported budget-alignment trends are robust to modeling choices.
\end{verbatim}

\subsection*{3. The synthetic experiment currently exposes a fundamental weakness of the method and is not adequately resolved}

\textbf{Problem.}  
Section IV-B.4 is the most revealing part of the paper, but also the most damaging in its current form. The paper states that on synthetic hierarchical data, the EMPA bias remains almost constant across different privacy budgets:
\[
\mathrm{Bias}_\epsilon \approx 0.894
\quad \text{for both} \quad
\epsilon=0.1 \text{ and } \epsilon=0.01.
\]
This is a major issue. If the method is intended to assess budget alignment, then a core output that is almost invariant to substantial perturbation changes is not serving that role effectively.

The paper correctly notes that the bias may reflect \emph{group structure} more than perturbation scale. However, this is not a side remark; it is a central challenge to the method's validity.

\textbf{Location.}  
Section IV-B.4, Table IV, Figure 7, Section IV-E(7).

\textbf{Why this matters technically.}  
A valid budget-alignment assessment quantity should show at least some monotonic sensitivity to perturbation strength under a controlled setup where grouping is fixed and only noise scale changes.

At minimum, one expects
\[
\epsilon_1 < \epsilon_2
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{BAS}_{\epsilon_1}]
\neq
\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{BAS}_{\epsilon_2}]
\]
under the chosen perturbation family. If the score instead satisfies approximately
\[
\mathrm{Bias}_{0.1} \approx \mathrm{Bias}_{0.01},
\]
then either the chosen statistic is poorly calibrated, or the grouping mechanism dominates the assessment.

\textbf{Required fix.}  
The authors must perform a controlled decomposition study. Specifically:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Fix the grouping and vary only the perturbation scale;
\item Fix the perturbation scale and vary only the grouping structure;
\item Report both $\mathrm{Bias}_\epsilon$ and full $\mathrm{BAS}_\epsilon$;
\item Determine whether the method is primarily a \emph{structure auditor} or a \emph{budget auditor}.
\end{enumerate}

Suggested TeX patch:

\begin{verbatim}
\paragraph*{Decomposition of structure and perturbation effects.}
To isolate the source of the EMPA discrepancy, we perform two
controlled studies. In the first, the sensitive/non-sensitive
partition is fixed and only the perturbation magnitude changes.
In the second, the perturbation family is fixed and only the
partition changes. Let
\[
\mathrm{BAS}_\epsilon^{\mathrm{fixed\mbox{-}group}}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\mathrm{BAS}^{\mathrm{fixed\mbox{-}noise}}(G)
\]
denote these two regimes. This decomposition clarifies whether
EMPA is primarily sensitive to perturbation scale or to induced
group structure.
\end{verbatim}

\subsection*{4. The baseline design remains weak and does not support strong performance claims}

\textbf{Problem.}  
The main quantitative baselines are Chi-square, K-L divergence, and MMD. These are generic discrepancy metrics, not budget-alignment methods. The manuscript explicitly acknowledges this in Section IV-A.3, which is honest, but it also means that the current results do not support any strong claim that Bodhi VLM is superior for the intended task.

The comparison against ViP, DP-Cap, DP-MTV, and VisShield in Table VI is also not technically meaningful as evidence of superiority, because these methods solve different problems:
\begin{itemize}
\item training-time DP representation learning,
\item multimodal DP task adaptation,
\item de-identification.
\end{itemize}
They are not post hoc budget-auditing methods.

\textbf{Location.}  
Section II-D, Section IV-A.3, Table VI, Table VII, Section V.

\textbf{Why this matters technically.}  
A TNNLS paper introducing a new assessment method should be evaluated against at least one \emph{task-matched comparator}. Otherwise, the empirical section demonstrates only that the proposed method differs from generic distances, not that it is the right tool for the stated problem.

\textbf{Required fix.}  
Add at least two task-relevant baselines:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a regression-based budget predictor from feature statistics,
\item a likelihood-based score under the known perturbation family.
\end{enumerate}

For example, define a feature-summary vector
\[
z = \psi(\tilde f, f, G^{(s)}, G^{(n)}),
\]
and fit a regressor
\[
\hat \epsilon = r_\theta(z).
\]
Then use
\[
\mathrm{Err}_\epsilon = |\hat \epsilon - \epsilon|
\]
or
\[
\mathrm{rMSE}_\epsilon
=
\sqrt{
\frac{1}{M}
\sum_{m=1}^{M}
(\hat\epsilon_m-\epsilon_m)^2
}.
\]

A second baseline is a perturbation-family likelihood score:
\[
\mathcal{L}_\epsilon
=
\sum_{j=1}^{N_s}
\log p(\tilde v_j - v_j \mid \epsilon).
\]

Suggested patch:

\begin{verbatim}
\paragraph*{Task-specific budget-alignment baselines.}
Beyond generic discrepancy measures, we introduce two
task-matched baselines. First, \emph{MomentReg} predicts the
declared budget $\epsilon$ from feature summary statistics
using a regression model
\[
\hat\epsilon = r_\theta(\psi(\tilde f,f,G^{(s)},G^{(n)})).
\]
Second, \emph{NoiseMLE} evaluates the log-likelihood of the
observed perturbation under the evaluator-specified additive
noise family:
\[
\mathcal{L}_\epsilon
=
\sum_{j=1}^{N_s}\log p(\tilde v_j-v_j\mid\epsilon).
\]
These baselines provide task-specific references for budget
alignment beyond generic distribution distances.
\end{verbatim}

\subsection*{5. The statistical protocol is still below the bar for TNNLS}

\textbf{Problem.}  
Section IV-A.4(a) explicitly states that, unless otherwise specified, values are from a single run and that mean$\pm$std or confidence intervals are not generally reported. This is a major methodological weakness. The paper involves stochastic perturbations, clustering, mixture fitting, and subset selection. Single-run reporting is not sufficient.

This concern is especially serious because some reported differences are small. For example, in Table V the BUA and TDA rMSE values for VLMs are very close:
\[
1.89 \text{ vs. } 1.92,\qquad
2.11 \text{ vs. } 2.08,\qquad
2.35 \text{ vs. } 2.41.
\]
Without uncertainty estimates, none of these differences are interpretable.

\textbf{Location.}  
Section IV-A.4(a), Table III, Table V, Table VII, Figures 3--8.

\textbf{Required fix.}  
Every major result table and plot should be based on repeated runs:
\[
\text{seeds} \in \{0,1,2,3,4\}
\]
at minimum. Report
\[
\mu \pm \sigma
\]
and where feasible, $95\%$ confidence intervals:
\[
\mu \pm 1.96 \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}.
\]
For small-sample evaluation, use bootstrap intervals.

Suggested patch:

\begin{verbatim}
\paragraph*{Statistical reporting protocol.}
All experiments are repeated over five random seeds
$\{0,1,2,3,4\}$, covering perturbation sampling, MDAV grouping,
subset selection, and EMPA initialization. We report
mean $\pm$ standard deviation in all tables and
95\% confidence intervals for the primary comparison plots.
Where pairwise method differences are discussed, we additionally
report the confidence interval of the matched-sample difference.
\end{verbatim}

\subsection*{6. The VLM experimental claim remains broader than the evidence}

\textbf{Problem.}  
The paper correctly states that it evaluates only the \emph{vision towers} of CLIP, LLaVA, and BLIP. However, the title, some parts of the abstract, and some conclusion language still suggest broader applicability to \emph{vision-language models} rather than to \emph{VLM visual encoders}. That distinction matters.

The paper does not evaluate:
\begin{enumerate}
\item cross-modal fusion,
\item image-text alignment behavior under privacy noise,
\item language decoding,
\item prompt-conditioned downstream sensitivity.
\end{enumerate}

Therefore, the current evidence supports transfer across \emph{visual backbones used inside VLMs}, not across full VLM systems.

\textbf{Location.}  
Title, Abstract, Section III-H, Table V, Table VI, Conclusion.

\textbf{Required fix.}  
Tighten the wording throughout so that the paper consistently states \emph{VLM vision encoders} or \emph{vision towers}. Also add at least one experiment probing cross-modal consistency if the authors wish to keep strong VLM language.

A minimal useful experiment would compute image-text similarity shifts in CLIP under perturbation:
\[
\Delta_{\mathrm{IT}}
=
\left|
\operatorname{sim}(g_I(\tilde x), g_T(t))
-
\operatorname{sim}(g_I(x), g_T(t))
\right|.
\]
This would at least connect the feature-level noise to a multimodal quantity.

\subsection*{7. The notation and algorithmic semantics are still not fully clean}

\textbf{Problem.}  
The paper is improved, but several notation issues remain:

\begin{enumerate}
\item $S_\epsilon$ still carries a misleading subscript $\epsilon$ despite being defined as a task-level sensitive concept set not learned from $\epsilon$.
\item Equation (5) uses set intersection across consecutive layers:
\[
L_i^{(s)} = G_i^{(s)} \cap G_{i-1}^{(s)},
\qquad
L_i^{(n)} = G_i^{(n)} \cap G_{i-1}^{(n)},
\]
but these groups live in different feature spaces unless a mapping is applied first.
\item Table I still includes $\Psi_i$ and $g_i$, which are not actually used in the final EMPA formulation.
\item The distinction between per-layer feature tensors, flattened vectors, pooled features, and common-space features is not explicit enough.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Location.}  
Table I, Section III-B, Section III-F, Algorithm 1, Algorithm 2, Eq. (5), Section III-H.

\textbf{Required fix.}  
First, rename
\[
S_\epsilon \to S
\]
throughout unless there is an actual budget-dependent concept set.

Second, make the inter-layer mapping explicit:
\[
\Pi_{i \to i-1} : X_i \to X_{i-1},
\]
and then redefine
\[
L_i^{(s)}
=
\Pi_{i \to i-1}(G_i^{(s)}) \cap G_{i-1}^{(s)}.
\]

Suggested patch:

\begin{verbatim}
\paragraph*{Cross-layer links in TDA.}
Because $G_i^{(s)}$ and $G_{i-1}^{(s)}$ generally lie in different
feature spaces, we first map higher-layer elements to the lower
layer via a correspondence operator
\[
\Pi_{i\to i-1}: X_i \rightarrow X_{i-1}.
\]
We then define
\[
L_i^{(s)} := \Pi_{i\to i-1}(G_i^{(s)}) \cap G_{i-1}^{(s)},
\qquad
L_i^{(n)} := \Pi_{i\to i-1}(G_i^{(n)}) \cap G_{i-1}^{(n)}.
\]
\end{verbatim}

Also remove unused notation from Table I.

\subsection*{8. The experimental protocol contains dataset and reporting inconsistencies}

\textbf{Problem.}  
There are several concrete experimental clarity problems:

\begin{enumerate}
\item ``COCO (Train2020/Val2020)'' is likely incorrect nomenclature; standard MS COCO splits are typically \texttt{train2017}/\texttt{val2017} or earlier versions.
\item ``MOT20 (150 frames)'' is not enough detail. Which sequences? Uniformly sampled or consecutive? Which split?
\item The perturbation model is described as scale proportional to $1/\epsilon$, but the exact calibration is not given:
\[
b = c/\epsilon \quad ?
\qquad
\sigma = c/\epsilon \quad ?
\]
with what constant $c$?
\item In Table II, the statement ``single-run scalar values'' conflicts with tables elsewhere that report mean$\pm$std over seeds.
\item Table III and Table VII report many values as
\[
0.00 \pm 0.00,
\]
which is suspicious without precision disclosure.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Location.}  
Section IV-A.1, Section IV-A.4, Table II, Table III, Table VII.

\textbf{Required fix.}  
Provide exact split names, sample counts, sequence IDs, sampling rules, perturbation calibration formula, numeric precision, and reconcile the single-run versus multi-seed reporting inconsistency.

A simple patch for perturbation calibration is:

\begin{verbatim}
\paragraph*{Perturbation calibration used in controlled experiments.}
For Laplace perturbation, we use
\[
u \sim \operatorname{Laplace}(0,b), \qquad b = c/\epsilon,
\]
and for Gaussian perturbation,
\[
u \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 I), \qquad \sigma = c/\epsilon,
\]
where $c$ is a fixed constant shared across all methods in a given
experiment. This controlled setup is used only to generate a
reference perturbation family for empirical auditing and is not
claimed as a formal DP calibration.
\end{verbatim}

\subsection*{9. The interpretation of ``competitive or lower rMSE'' is not convincing as currently written}

\textbf{Problem.}  
In multiple places, the paper states that EMPA achieves \emph{competitive or lower} budget-alignment error than Chi-square, K-L divergence, and MMD. However, the actual numbers do not consistently support a strong narrative.

For example, in Table III:
\begin{itemize}
\item Chi-square rMSE is approximately $0.35$--$0.36$,
\item EMPA is $0.89$ on several models,
\item MMD is $0.00$--$0.00$.
\end{itemize}
Even if MMD is not a task-specific baseline, such results make the claim difficult to read at face value.

This suggests either:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the metrics are not directly comparable,
\item the reported targets are different,
\item or the claim wording is too strong.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Location.}  
Abstract, Section IV-B.3, Section V, Table III, Table VII.

\textbf{Required fix.}  
Either normalize all metrics to the same target and scale, or soften the claims substantially. For instance, replace ``competitive or lower'' with language such as:

\begin{verbatim}
EMPA provides a stable feature-level discrepancy signal under the
reported setups; however, its numerical values are not directly
comparable to generic distribution distances without a shared
normalization target.
\end{verbatim}

\subsection*{10. The ablation study is too weak for a paper of this scope}

\textbf{Problem.}  
The ablation in Table VIII includes only:
\begin{itemize}
\item w/o BUA,
\item w/o TDA,
\item w/o EMPA.
\end{itemize}
This is not enough. Since the method depends on NCP, MDAV, thresholding, mixture modeling, and grouping rules, the ablation should test the sensitivity of each core design choice.

\textbf{Location.}  
Section IV-C, Table VIII.

\textbf{Required fix.}  
At minimum, add:
\begin{enumerate}
\item w/o MDAV,
\item w/o NCP,
\item threshold percentile variation
\[
\tau_i \in \{80\%, 90\%, 95\%\},
\]
\item mixture component variation
\[
K \in \{2,4,8\},
\]
\item random grouping versus structured grouping on real data, not only synthetic data.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Minor Comments}

\begin{enumerate}
\item The acronym expansion of Bodhi VLM in the abstract is somewhat forced and does not fully match the actual method content.
\item The phrase ``links sensitive data labels to privacy budgets via clustering'' in the abstract is still vague and potentially misleading.
\item The paper should explicitly define the feature pooling rule when sensitive vectors from multiple layers are aggregated into
\[
V^{(s)} = \{v_j\}_{j=1}^{N_s}.
\]
\item Table II should not state vague hardware like ``RTX/A100 class''; list exact GPU model(s).
\item The references contain several recent arXiv citations that should be checked for precise versions and correctness.
\item The BLIP omission in Table VII should be explained more carefully; a missing model weakens the completeness of the VLM section.
\item PSNR and SIFT remain weakly motivated for this problem and may distract from the main technical story.
\item The paper should clearly mark when results are on synthetic data versus real detector/VLM features, because this distinction is easy to lose in the current narrative.
\item Figure captions should be much more self-contained. Each caption should include dataset, sample size, perturbation family, declared $\epsilon$, aggregation rule, and whether lower is better.
\item Several reported results appear over-precise relative to the limited statistical analysis.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Explicit Audit Against Common TNNLS Failure Points}

\subsection*{Claim--Evidence Alignment}
\textbf{Assessment: improved but still imperfect.}  
The manuscript is substantially better because it now limits itself to feature-level auditing under controlled perturbations. However, some remaining wording still implies stronger VLM-level generality than the evidence supports.

\subsection*{Notation Hygiene}
\textbf{Assessment: moderate, but still needs work.}  
The notation is more coherent than before, but not yet clean enough for effortless verification. In particular, $S_\epsilon$, Eq.~(5), and unused Table I symbols need revision.

\subsection*{Algorithm Reproducibility}
\textbf{Assessment: partial.}  
Algorithms 1 and 2 are readable, but still not fully executable without consulting multiple later sections. A reproducible paper should specify exact data objects, mapping operators, and aggregation rules in one place.

\subsection*{Mathematical Defensibility}
\textbf{Assessment: moderate at best.}  
The paper correctly avoids formal theorems it cannot prove. However, EMPA remains more heuristic than principled. This is acceptable only if the manuscript fully embraces its empirical nature and provides stronger robustness evidence.

\subsection*{Metric Definition Clarity}
\textbf{Assessment: insufficient.}  
The current manuscript still mixes deviation, rMSE, BAS, and Bias without a single unifying metric paragraph.

\subsection*{Baseline Adequacy}
\textbf{Assessment: weak.}  
This is still one of the biggest problems.

\subsection*{Statistical Robustness}
\textbf{Assessment: inadequate.}  
This remains a must-fix issue.

\subsection*{Sensitivity-Set Definition}
\textbf{Assessment: better, but still somewhat subjective.}  
The paper now defines sensitive concept sets per dataset, which is good. However, a transferability or sensitivity analysis over the choice of $S$ is still missing.

\subsection*{Controlled Perturbation vs. Real Mechanisms}
\textbf{Assessment: appropriately acknowledged.}  
The limitation is now stated clearly in the manuscript. This is a strength. The remaining need is to make sure no later claim contradicts it.

\subsection*{Reproducibility Details}
\textbf{Assessment: partial.}  
The paper is improved, but still not at the level where an external reader could reproduce all experiments with no ambiguity.

\subsection*{Figure/Table Self-Contained Captions}
\textbf{Assessment: insufficient.}  
Captions are still too light for a TNNLS paper.

\section*{Minimum Bar for Acceptance}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Clearly define the \textbf{primary target of estimation} and distinguish it from secondary diagnostics.
\item Resolve the \textbf{synthetic-study failure mode} showing that EMPA bias is insensitive to $\epsilon$.
\item Add \textbf{task-matched baselines} beyond Chi-square, K-L, and MMD.
\item Redo the main experiments with \textbf{multi-seed statistical reporting}.
\item Tighten the paper to consistently claim transfer only to \textbf{VLM vision encoders}, unless multimodal experiments are added.
\item Fix the \textbf{cross-layer mapping semantics} in TDA and clean the notation.
\item Expand the \textbf{ablation study} to cover MDAV, NCP, thresholding, and mixture-model design.
\item Resolve the \textbf{dataset/protocol inconsistencies} and improve the captions/tables substantially.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Optional Enhancements}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Add a cross-modal CLIP image--text consistency experiment.
\item Add calibration curves showing how $\mathrm{BAS}_\epsilon$ changes under fixed grouping.
\item Analyze runtime and memory overhead of BUA/TDA/EMPA.
\item Include a transferability study across different sensitive concept sets $S$.
\item Compare alternative density models beyond Gaussian mixtures.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Final Verdict}

This paper has a plausible and potentially useful idea, and it has clearly improved in scope control and honesty about limitations. However, for \emph{IEEE TNNLS}, the current version still falls short in methodological rigor, baseline strength, and statistical validation. I would encourage the authors to continue this work, but the manuscript needs a substantial revision before it can meet the standard of a top-tier journal.
